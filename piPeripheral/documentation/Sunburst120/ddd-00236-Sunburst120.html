I'm unaware of any surviving documentation related specifically to telemetry for the SUNBURST program.  Or indeed little else about SUNBURST.  Consequently, most of the information herein is derived from SUNBURST source code and analogies to less-closely-related software such as SUNDANCE, AURORA, and LUMINARY, and one mustn't place too much confidence in it.

Go directly to word: 10 20 30 40 50 60 70 80 90

1a

I.D. word for this list.  Will contain 002368.

1b

Sync bits.  Will contain 004378.

2-8

RN, +2, +4, VN, +2, +4, STATIME, +1.  LM STATE VECTOR and TIME.  The LGC's latest calculated state vector for the LM in reference coordinates.  The coordinates may be either earth-centered or moon-centered; a zero in bit 11 of flagword 8 (LMOONFLG) indicates earth-centered, a one indicates moon-centered.  Words 2-4 contain the position components X, Y, Z scaled meters/229.  Words 5-7 contain the velocity components X, Y, Z scaled (meters/centisecond)/27.  Words 8-9 contain the double-precision time associated with the state vector, referenced to the computer clock.  The scaling for position, velocity and time is the same whether earth-centered or moon-centered.  These parameters are calculated whenever the LM state vector is permanently extrapolated or changed.

9a

Garbage

9b,10a,10b

CDUXD, CDUYD, CDUZD. DAP INTERNAL DESIRED CDUs (X, Y, Z).  Outer, inner and middle intermediate desired gimbal angles.  Steering commands to DAP.  Generated during a burn by guidance equations or during an automatic maneuver.  Calculated every 2 secs during a PGNCS controlled burn (i.e., P40, P42).  Updated every 0.1 sec by the DAP (using DELCDUs) during an automatic maneuver (KALCMANU) and also during powered flight.  These registers are unsigned 15-bit fractions.  Scaled degrees/360.

11-13

DELVX, DELVY, DELVZ.  The accumulated PIPA counts when the servicer is running.  At the start of each 2-second cycle the PIPAs are zeroed and the contents of the PIPAs are loaded into DELVs.  In stable member coordinates.  Scaled (centimeters/second)/214.

14-17

LMPCMD, +1, ..., +7.  This is a circular buffer of up to 8 commands to be sent from the AGC to the LEM Mission Programmer (LMP, not to be confused with Lunar Module Pilot).  (See also words 18a,18b.)  Buffering is needed because physical output to the LMP only takes place upon overflow of timer TIME4, which occurs at 10 ms. intervals.  Recall that in unmanned Apollo missions, the LMP performed certain functions within the LEM that the absent astronaut would otherwise have performed in manned missions. The 8 least-significant bits determine the particular command. Refer to the Lunar Module Systems Handbook for AS-206/LM-1, Table 13-4 for a list of all possible commands, or Table 13-5 for more-detailed descriptions of some of them. 

18a,18b

LMPIN, LMPOUT.  These are indices into the LMPCMD circular buffer.  (See words 14-17.)  LMPIN is the index (0-7) in LMPCMD at which the next command added to the buffer will be stored, while LMPOUT is the next index from which to remove a command from LMPCMD for actually output the physical LMP. 

19a

ALPHAR.  ALPHAP, ALPHAQ, and ALPHAR are the calculated angular acceleration about the X, Y, and Z body axes due to the fact that the main engine is not exactly aligned with the center of gravity.  During powered flight these are calculated every 0.1 second at the beginning of the DAP cycle; otherwise these are zeroed in every DAP cycle.  Also zeroed at discrete times, such as fresh start and engine off.  Scaled (degrees/second2)/90.  Expected range is ±35 degrees/second2. 

Aside: But what about ALPHAP and ALPHAQ?  ALPHAQ is emitted later (see "garbage" word 38a), while the SUNBURST source code notes that "ALPHAP HAS NO USE IN THE DIGITAL AUTOPILOT", and thus apparently as something even worse than the garbagey ALPHAQ is not emitted in the downlist at all.

19b

Garbage.

20b

TEVENT, +1.  TIME OF EVENT.  Contains the image of TIME2, TIME1 at the time of the last significant event.  Loaded in burn programs with time of ignition and time of engine cutoff as they occur.  Loaded with abort initiation time in P70 and P71.  Scaled centiseconds/228.

31

FC, +1. GUIDANCE THRUST COMMAND (FC).  The magnitude of the desired DPS thrust computed by the Lunar Landing Guidance Equations.  It does not apply to Ascent or Aborts.  Calculated once every 2 seconds during landing.  Scaled lbs/(2.817 × 214).

32a,32b

AOSQ, AOSR.  These are ascent offset acceleration estimates, estimated every 2 seconds by AOSTASK.

Aside:  It was in writing the blurb above that I realized — after over 20 years working with this material! — that I do not know what the P, Q, and R axes are, even though they appear quite often.  For example, see below.  The explanation isn't directly written down in any reference I have been able to discover.  The assumption is that everybody just seems to know what they are, and heaven help those who do not.  But if you're diligent enough in searching AGC source code, you do find a passing mention that axes P, Q, and R are "pilot axes", which you'd likely presume are somehow related to the pilot's point-of-view concerning front-back, right-left, and up-down, though not necessarily in that order.  There's also passing mention that (at least in the command module), "the body axes XB,YB,ZB are normally designated P,Q,R."  A variety of "standard" coordinate systems for Apollo, though not any "pilot" coordinate system, are in fact documented:
  • "Project Apollo Coordinate System Standards"
  • GSOP for Colossus 3
  • GSOP for Luminary 1E
  • etc.
The XB,YB,ZB system mentioned above would seem to be the one discussed in 5.1.4.3 ("Vehicle or Body Coordinate System") of the Luminary GSOP, from which we'd tentatively infer that:
  • The P (XB) axis points "upward" through the LM-to-CM transfer tunnel.  I.e., it corresponds roughly to pilot up/down ("up" being the positive direction).
  • The R (ZB) axis points outward through the LM's exit hatch, corresponding to pilot right/left ("left" being the positive direction).
  • The Q (YB) axis completes the triad, so that P,Q,R is right-handed ... which, it would appear to me, means that Q is roughly parallel to the pilot's viewline through the window, thus corresponding to front/back ("front" being the positive direction).

REDOCTR.  Counter for hardware restarts.  Set to zero by keyboard freshstart (Verb 36).  Incremented once per hardware restart by restart program.

SFAIL. Address of a built-in self-test routine that failed.

Garbage.

35b,36a,36b

CDUX (outer gimbal) CDUY (inner gimbal) CDUZ (middle gimbal): The actual values of the IMU CDU angles. Each register is an unsigned 15-bit fraction, scaled degrees/360, and is updated by counter interrupts. A middle gimbal angle greater than 70 degrees will light the Gimbal Lock lamp on the DSKY. Whenever the magnitude of the MGA exceeds 85 degrees the ISS stabilization loop will be disabled. As a result, the CDUZ register should maintain values that correspond to middle gimbal angles between -70 degrees and +70 degrees.

OMEGAP, OMEGAQ, Garbage, OMEGAR.  CURRENT BODY RATES (X, Y, Z).  Estimated current rates about body axis.  Calculated at the beginning of every DAP cycle every 0.1 sec.  Zeroed at DAP start-up.  Scaled (degrees/sec)/45.  Expected range of ±10 degrees/sec but ±45 degrees/sec possible.

If bits 15 through 12 of DSPTAB+11D are 1000, the last 11 bits indicate the state to which the CMC will command the relays; if bits 15 through 12 are 0000, the last 11 bits indicate the actual state of the relays.  A one indicates that the discrete is on.

51

TIME2, TIME1.  Double precision word indicating ground elapsed time.  Used for all timing while CMC is on.  Zeroed at liftoff and incremented by one bit per centisecond. Scaled centiseconds/228.

52-54

COMPTORK, +1, ..., +4.  V, S, E gyrocompass gyro torques.  I.e., value of required number of gyro torquing pulses (before rotation by [Xsm], and hence in the vertical, south, east system), scale factor B21, units pulses.

55a,55b,56a

FAILREG, +1, +2.  A set of three single-precision cells used to retain alarm pattern code information.  They are all reset to 0 by a fresh start.  FAILREG and FAILREG+1 are also reset to 0 by use of the "ERROR RESET" keycode.  FAILREG contains the first alarm code received after the "ERROR RESET", FAILREG+1 contains the second, and FAILREG+2 will always contain the most recent alarm code.  Octal quantities.

56b,57a,57b

ALMCADR, +1, +2. 

58a,58b,59a

PIPAX, PIPAY, PIPAZ. These are CPU special registers (addresses 37-41 octal).  They contain the accumulation of output pulses from the X,Y, and Z accelerometers (a measure of the velocity changes), scaled (centimeters/second)/(5.85 × 214).

DELAREA, +1.  As far as I can tell from the comments in the SUNBURST 120 source code, one of the factors that SUNBURST attempts to take into account in tracking the current mass of the LEM is the loss of mass from the throat of the engine as it erodes.  DELAREA appears to be an estimation of the current loss of engine throat-area, starting at 0 and increasing thereafter.  The units are %, scaled 27.

MPHASE4, MPHASE3, MPHASE2, MPHASE1, MTIMER4, MTIMER3, MTIMER2, MTIMER1. This is supposedly documented in Chapter 4 of the AS-206 (Apollo 5) GSOP, which we unfortunately do not have.  Here's what I think is happening.  MTIMERx contains a time at which some mission phase is supposed to start, while MPHASEx holds the associated mission-phase number.  When one of the times is hit, the associated phase is initiated, and MPHASEx/MTIMERx are then free to be reused for some other mission phase.  (Feel free to wade through the source-code file AS206_MISSION_SCHEDULING_PACKAGE.agc to refine this description.)
